TDEI — Temporal Demand Elasticity Index
Decomposes annual demand into Fourier components — capturing smooth seasonal waves, holiday proximity spikes, and day-of-week effects. Unlike a calendar lookup table, TDEI produces a continuous score (0–1) that feeds directly into the optimisation engine. Above 0.75 triggers surge pricing; below 0.35 activates discount bundles.
What to try
Annual amplitude (α₁): Controls depth of the seasonal wave — high values create a strong summer/winter contrast.  ·  Holiday spike (δ): How much a public holiday amplifies demand — watch new peaks form at holiday weeks.  ·  Decay rate (λ): How quickly the holiday effect fades — low values spread it over many weeks; high values make it sharp and brief. Watch how the number of surge weeks and stimulation weeks changes — these determine how many weeks per year PRISM operates in premium vs. discount mode.

Dimension 2 of 5  ·  β₂ ≈ 0.24
WAAP — Weather-Adjusted Attendance Probability
A logistic regression model that outputs the probability a ticket-holder actually shows up, conditional on weather forecast. The critical innovation is the interaction term: rain probability × outdoor fraction. A 60% rain forecast matters far more for an 85%-outdoor park than a 30%-outdoor one. WAAP learns the park's own weather sensitivity profile.
What to try
Rain probability: Drag from 0% to 100% and watch P(attend) fall — the rate depends on outdoor fraction.  ·  Temperature deviation: Extremes reduce attendance independently of rain.  ·  Outdoor fraction: The key interaction term — change it from 30% to 85% and observe how "This park" separates from the other curves. The pricing action in the interpretation box switches automatically at P(attend) thresholds of 0.80, 0.60, and 0.40.

Dimension 3 of 5  ·  β₃ ≈ 0.21
VSPSS — Visitor Segment Price Sensitivity Score
k-means clustering on behavioural features (booking lead time, origin distance, visit frequency, group size) identifies four latent visitor archetypes. A constant-elasticity demand curve is fit per segment. Each segment has its own ε and revenue-maximising price p*. One uniform price misprices every segment simultaneously.
What to read here
Left chart (demand curves): Steeper slope = more elastic = visitor is price-sensitive. Segment A (Pilgrim) barely responds; Segment D (Impulse Group) drops sharply.  ·  Right chart (revenue curves): Each segment has a distinct revenue peak — that peak is p*. The wider the spread of p* values, the more value is unlocked by per-segment pricing vs. one uniform price.

Module 2 of 3  ·  Stage 1 → Stage 2 pipeline
Two-Stage Optimisation Engine
Stage 1 forecasts demand Q(t, k) per time slot per visitor segment using all five dimension scores as features. Stage 2 solves a constrained non-linear problem (SLSQP) to find the price vector that maximises total RevPAX subject to six real-world constraints: market position, queue experience, price stability, and a fairness guardrail.
What to try
TDEI score: Drag toward 1.0 to simulate a peak holiday — watch prices rise and the revenue surface shift right.  ·  WAAP score: Drag toward 0 for heavy rain — prices fall as expected attendance drops.  ·  CPI ceiling: The market constraint — lower it and watch bars to the right go grey (infeasible), forcing the optimiser to select a lower price. The highlighted purple bar is always the Stage 2 solution: revenue-maximising price that satisfies all constraints.
