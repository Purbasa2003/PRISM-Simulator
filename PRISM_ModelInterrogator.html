TDEI — Temporal Demand Elasticity Index
Decomposes annual demand into its Fourier components — capturing smooth seasonal waves, holiday proximity spikes, and day-of-week effects. Unlike a calendar lookup table, TDEI produces a continuous score from 0 to 1 that feeds directly into the optimisation engine as a numeric feature. When TDEI exceeds 0.75, surge pricing is triggered. Below 0.35, demand stimulation bundles activate.
What to try
Annual amplitude (α₁): Controls the depth of the seasonal wave — high values create a pronounced summer/winter contrast.  ·  Holiday spike (δ): How much a public holiday amplifies demand — watch new peaks form at holiday weeks.  ·  Decay rate (λ): How quickly the holiday spike falls away — low values spread a holiday's effect over many weeks, high values make it sharp and brief. Observe how the number of surge weeks and stimulation weeks changes — these directly determine how many weeks of the year PRISM operates in premium vs. discount mode.

WAAP — Weather-Adjusted Attendance Probability
A logistic regression model that outputs the probability a ticket-holder actually shows up, conditional on weather forecast. The critical innovation is the interaction term: rain probability × outdoor fraction. A 60% rain forecast matters far more for a park that is 85% outdoor rides than one with 30%. WAAP learns the park's own weather sensitivity profile — not generic "rain = bad" logic.
What to try
Rain probability: Drag from 0% to 100% and watch P(attend) fall — the rate of fall depends on the outdoor fraction.  ·  Temperature deviation: Hot or cold extremes reduce attendance independently of rain.  ·  Outdoor fraction: This is the key interaction term — change it from 30% to 85% and observe how the "This park" curve separates from the others on the chart. The pricing action (shown in the interpretation box) switches automatically at P(attend) thresholds of 0.80, 0.60, and 0.40.

Dimension 3 of 5  ·  β₃ ≈ 0.21
VSPSS — Visitor Segment Price Sensitivity Score
k-means clustering on behavioural features (booking lead time, origin distance, visit frequency, group size) identifies four latent visitor archetypes. A constant-elasticity demand curve is then fit per segment. The result: each segment has its own price elasticity ε and its own revenue-maximising price p*. One uniform price across all segments leaves money on the table from inelastic visitors and drives away elastic ones.
What to read here
Left chart (demand curves): Steeper slope = more elastic = visitor is more sensitive to price increases. Segment A (Pilgrim) barely responds to price — a nearly flat curve. Segment D (Impulse Group) drops sharply — they will leave if priced above their threshold.  ·  Right chart (revenue curves): Each segment has a distinct revenue peak — this is p*, the revenue-maximising price for that segment. The wider the spread of p* values across segments, the more value is unlocked by per-segment pricing vs. a single price. The formula below shows how p* is derived directly from ε and marginal cost.

Formula — constant elasticity demand + revenue-maximising price
Q_k(p) = α_k · p^(ε_k) ← constant elasticity demand curve per segment k Revenue: R_k(p) = p · Q_k(p) Revenue-maximising price: p*_k = ε_k · MC / (ε_k − 1) ← derived from dR/dp = 0, where MC = marginal cost
Module 2 of 3  ·  Stage 1 → Stage 2 pipeline
Two-Stage Optimisation Engine
Stage 1 uses XGBoost/LightGBM to forecast demand Q(t, k) per time slot per visitor segment, using all five dimension scores as features. Stage 2 takes that forecast as input and solves a constrained non-linear optimisation problem (SLSQP) to find the price vector that maximises total RevPAX — subject to six real-world constraints including market position (CPI), queue experience, price stability, and a segment fairness guardrail.
What to try
TDEI score: Drag toward 1.0 to simulate a peak holiday period — watch base price and segment prices rise, and the revenue surface shift right toward a higher optimal price.  ·  WAAP score: Drag toward 0 to simulate a heavy rain forecast — prices adjust downward as expected attendance falls.  ·  CPI ceiling: This is the market constraint — it caps how far above competitor prices PRISM will go. Lower it and watch bars to the right of the ceiling go grey (infeasible), forcing the optimiser to select a lower price. The highlighted purple bar is always the Stage 2 solution — the revenue-maximising price that satisfies all constraints simultaneously.
